Was there a conspiracy to kill President Kennedy?

Lee Harvey Oswald protested his innocence even as he lay dying.

Summary

From the moment shots rang out at lunchtime on November 22nd 1963, talk of conspiracy surrounded the assassination of President John F Kennedy.

A government commission headed by Judge Earl Warren concluded in 1964 that a mysterious ex marine — Lee Harvey Oswald, committed the assassination alone, firing 3 shots from the 6th floor of the Texas School Book Depository. They were unable to provide any credible motive and much of their evidence was soon discredited by critics.

A second government commission — the HSCA, concluded in 1979 that President Kennedy was ‘was probably assassinated as a result of a conspiracy’, but its evidence proved equally controversial and contentious.

The question still remains more than 50 years on — was President Kennedy killed as the result of a conspiracy?

Evidence for the theory

Eyewitness testimony shows Oswald wasn't on the 6th floor at the time of the assassination


Multiple eyewitnesses contradict the official story that Oswald was the assassin. Arnold Roland saw two men on the 6th floor just 15 minutes before the assassination. In order to escape after the shots, Oswald would have had to run down a staircase past secretaries Victoria Adams and Sandra Style, but neither saw him. Likewise caretaker Jack Dougherty was stood on a stairwell that Oswald would have had to run down after the shooting and never saw him.


A policeman — Marian Baker, claims to have encountered Oswald in a 2nd floor lunchtime just 70–90 seconds after the shots, looking calm and composed. Could he really have gotten there in time? Oswald’s presence there was in fact consistent with several other witnesses who placed Oswald on the lower floors of the Texas School Book depository when he should have been on the 6th floor preparing his snipers nest.

Oswald yelled ‘I am not resisting arrest’ after he was apprehended at the Texas Theatre.

The description of the assassin does not match Oswald

Multiple eyewitnesses who saw a man at the 6th floor window during the assassination describe him as wearing light or khaki coloured clothes. Oswald, however, was wearing a dark red shirt and dark pants on the day of the assassination.

The official story is emphatically contradicted by the medical evidence

The medical evidence provided the official ‘lone gunman’ scenario with problems from the beginning. The Warren Commision concluded that Oswald fired 3 shots — the first missed and the second hit Kennedy in the upper back, passing out through his neck and going on to cause all of the wounds to Governor Connally. After a 5 second or so gap, the third and last bullet was the fatal head shot that killed The President.

Yet everyone present at the autopsy on the night of the assassination was certain the wound in the Presidents back was shallow and did not transit the body. Also, the trajectory of this shot never lined up — the Warren Commision made it fit by moving the true location of the back wound, and later attempts to prove the theory simply lied about the position of the two men’s bodies in the limousine.

Other evidence shows there were 2 bullet wounds in Kennedy’s head rather than 1. And the vast majority of the witnesses in Dealey Plaza were certain the last 2 shots were very closely bunched together — not the 5 second gap of the official story, and faster than Oswald's rifle could actually refire.

Tests conducted on Oswald’s face showed he hadn't fired a rifle that day

A simple paraffin test was administered to Oswald’s cheek after his arrest. After this showed negative for gunshot residue, the FBI submitted the cast to a whole array of more advanced tests, all of which returned negative. This was so problematic for the official case that the FBI simply lied about it at the Warren Commission's hearings. Whilst this evidence has caveats in some areas, it is still strongly suggestive of Oswald’s innocence.

Oswald was involved with the intelligence services

Oswald’s life is replete with mysteries and inexplicable events. Far from being a disaffected communist sympathizing loner, Oswald continually associated with right wingers and members of various intelligence agencies, including the FBI and the CIA.

Strange facts surrounding Oswald’s defection to the USSR in 1959, and the way he so easily returned to the USA in 1962, are indicative of his involvement in an intelligence operation, as is his fluency in Russian.

In New Orleans in the summer of 1963, Oswald was seen in the presence of multiple individuals involved in counter intelligence operations against leftist groups, and was seemingly involved in agent provocateur activity against the Fair Play For Cuba Committee.

Most suggestive of all, just 6 weeks before the assassination, Oswald was involved — either knowingly or not, in a complex intelligence operation in Mexico City where he was impersonated on at least 1 occasion. The purpose of this was seemingly to try and associate him with both Cuban and Soviet intelligence, and use the spectre of their involvement in Kennedy’s murder to frighten government agencies into participating in the cover-up.

Evidence against the theory

Oswald’s behaviour

Oswald fled the Texas School Book depository immediately after the assassination and during an attempt to escape the area, allegedly shot dead a policeman. Oswald was later captured in the Texas Theatre after a scuffle with police officers. These actions are indicative of some kind of guilt, but like much of the evidence presented against Oswald, it is tendentious and could equally have other valid explanations — for instance Oswald may have fled the scene because he realised he had been set up and feared for his life.
